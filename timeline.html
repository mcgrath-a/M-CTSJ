In this scenario, you directly engage with Judith Butler’s concept of gender performativity. In “ Performative Acts and Gender Constitution: An Essay in Phenomenology and Feminist Theory,” Butler articulates that gender is not an inherent identity but "the legacy of sedimented acts" that are neither "natural, cultural, nor linguistic." Essentially, Butler argues that gender is not something we are born with but something we perform and construct through our behaviors and social interactions. By intentionally flipping the expected gender of a doctor, you contribute to redefining these acts that form our understanding of professional roles. Butler suggests that such individual acts "do work to maintain and reproduce systems of oppression" but crucially adds that "oppression is not solely a consequence of these acts." This perspective highlights the complexity of gender dynamics, where changing deep-seated biases requires not just altering individual actions but transforming the broader "hegemonic social conditions" that give rise to them. Butler warns that focusing solely on individual actions without addressing underlying social conditions risks only touching upon the "indirect, if not epiphenomenal, reflection of those conditions."
